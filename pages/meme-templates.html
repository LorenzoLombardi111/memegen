Popular meme formats explained

If you are new to meme culture or just want to understand which template works best for your idea, here is a breakdown of the most popular formats:

Drake format

Two panels stacked vertically. The top panel shows rejection or disapproval, the bottom shows approval or preference. Use it for any comparison where one option is clearly better (or funnier) than the other. It is one of the most versatile formats ever created.

Expanding Brain

A multi-panel vertical format with increasingly elaborate brain images. Each panel escalates in absurdity. Use it when you want to show a progression from a basic idea to an over-the-top genius (or ridiculous) alternative. Works well with three, four, or five panels.

Two Panel

The simplest meme structure. Left and right, or top and bottom. One panel sets up the context, the other delivers the punchline. Two-panel templates work when your joke is clean and direct.

Four Panel (2x2 grid)

A comic-strip layout arranged in a square. Ideal for sequential storytelling, cause-and-effect humor, or showing multiple perspectives on the same topic. The four-panel grid gives you enough space to develop an idea without losing the viewer.

Gradient and solid backgrounds

Not every meme needs an image. Sometimes bold text on a colorful gradient or a plain background delivers the message more effectively. These templates are great for quotes, hot takes, announcements, and text-heavy humor.

How to choose the right template for your meme

The best template depends on what kind of joke or message you are trying to deliver:

  • Binary choice or comparison? Use Drake or a two-panel template.
  • Escalating absurdity or tier ranking? Expanding Brain is the clear pick.
  • A short story or sequence? Go with a four-panel grid.
  • Just text, no image needed? Pick a gradient or solid background.
  • Using your own image? Upload it directly and skip the gallery.

If you are unsure, start with the Drake format. It is the most flexible and widely understood meme structure. Once you are comfortable, experiment with more complex layouts like Expanding Brain or custom multi-panel designs.
